# One-Page Cheat Sheet

The brain remembers structure better than paragraphs. A wall of prose is useless
when you have five minutes before an exam, an interview, a meeting, or a real task.
This skill compresses a topic into one scannable page built for *fast recall* — the
learner should be able to skim it and have the whole topic snap back into place.

This skill owns the **Consolidation** pillar: a condensed reference that makes
review efficient. It's a compression tool, not a teaching tool — it assumes the
learner has already met the material and makes it retrievable in minutes. If
they're learning the topic for the first time, point them to **learning-ladder**
or **twenty-hours** instead.

## Before you build

One question matters most: **what's the moment of use?** An interview cheat sheet
emphasizes "why" and trade-offs; an exam sheet emphasizes formulas and definitions;
a hands-on-task sheet emphasizes steps and gotchas. Ask, then bias the sheet toward
that moment.

## Output: the cheat sheet

Act as an expert teacher who simplifies complex ideas into a fast review sheet. Fit
it on one page and include:

1. **A one-line definition** of the topic in plain language.
2. **The most important concepts, rules, formulas, or steps** — the core that does
   the heavy lifting.
3. **Bullets, not paragraphs.** Everything scannable. If a sentence runs long,
   it's probably two bullets.
4. **A visual if it helps** — a small table, a flowchart in text, a labeled mental
   model, or a decision tree. Use it when relationships matter more than facts.
5. **3–5 concrete examples** that show the topic working in real life. Examples are
   what make abstract rules retrievable.
6. **Common mistakes / confusing parts** to avoid — the traps that bite under pressure.
7. **A "Before You Use This" checklist** — the quick gut-check before acting.
8. **5 rapid-fire questions** to test memory, with brief answers tucked at the end.

## What makes a cheat sheet good

- **It fits the format.** If it spills past a page, cut. Compression is the value;
  a three-page "cheat sheet" is just notes.
- **It's scannable in 5 minutes.** The learner should find any item in seconds.
  Group related things; lead each section with its most important item.
- **Examples over explanation.** When forced to choose between explaining a rule
  and showing it, show it.
- **It targets the moment of use.** A sheet that helps "in general" helps less than
  one tuned to the interview/exam/task they named.

Keep it practical, visual, beginner-friendly, and easy to scan.

## Format note

Default to clean Markdown so it's easy to copy, print, or paste into notes. If the
learner wants a printable or polished artifact, offer to render it (e.g. a PDF or a
formatted one-pager) rather than leaving it as raw text.
