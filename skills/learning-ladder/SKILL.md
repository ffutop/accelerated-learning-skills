# Learning Ladder

Most people fail at learning because they jump into advanced material before the
basics are solid, then get discouraged when nothing makes sense. The fix is a
visible path: clear levels, each with a milestone that *proves* readiness before
moving up. The learner should never have to wonder "am I ready for the next
thing yet?" — the ladder answers that.

This skill owns the **Sequence** pillar of learning: a defined order in which each
level builds on the prerequisites below it, so nothing is attempted before its
foundation is solid.

## Before you build

Get just enough context to make the ladder real instead of generic:

- What's the topic, specifically? ("Learn Python" → for data analysis? web? automation?)
- Why are they learning it — and what does "good enough" look like for them?
- Any relevant background, even adjacent? (knowing some JS changes a Rust ladder)

If they don't answer, build a sensible default ladder and say what you assumed.

## Output: the 5-level ladder

Act as an expert teacher and skill coach. Break the topic into **5 difficulty
levels**, novice to expert. Use these level names as the spine — express the
names in the learner's language; the English here is just the reference:

- **Level 1: Novice**
- **Level 2: Advanced Beginner**
- **Level 3: Competent**
- **Level 4: Proficient**
- **Level 5: Expert**

For **each level**, include all eight of these — they're what make a ladder
useful rather than a table of contents:

1. Level name
2. What I should understand at this stage
3. What mastery looks like at this level
4. The most important concepts or skills to focus on
5. One milestone that proves I am ready to move forward
6. One hands-on exercise or mini-project
7. Common mistakes learners make at this level
8. A simple self-check question before moving to the next level

## What makes a ladder good

- **Milestones are demonstrations, not feelings.** "Build a working X" beats "you
  understand X." If they can do the milestone, they've earned the next rung.
- **Each level earns the next.** This is the sequence rule made concrete: Level 3
  should be impossible to fault at without Level 2's foundation. If a level could
  be skipped, the ladder is wrong.
- **Exercises are finishable.** A beginner mini-project that takes 20 hours isn't
  a mini-project. Scale ambition to the level.
- **Concrete to the topic.** Name real tools, real first projects, real common
  errors for *this* subject — not interchangeable filler.

Keep it practical and beginner-friendly. The goal is real progress, not an
impressive-looking syllabus.

## After the ladder

Point the learner to the natural next steps:

- To pick high-quality resources for the climb → **essential-five**.
- To turn the early levels into a time-boxed plan → **twenty-hours**.
- After studying a level, to test if it really stuck → **quiz-me**.
