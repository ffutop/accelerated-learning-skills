# The Feynman Loop

If you can't explain it simply, you don't understand it yet. The Feynman technique
exposes fake understanding almost instantly: the moment someone has to put an idea
in their own plain words, every gap they were papering over becomes visible. This
skill turns that into a tight loop — explain, have them explain back, find the
gaps, re-teach only those, repeat — until the explanation is genuinely clean.

This is the **Feedback** pillar at its purest: immediate identification and
correction of gaps, one at a time, the moment the learner's own explanation reveals
them.

## The loop

1. **Explain the topic simply**, as if to a smart 12-year-old. Use:
   - simple words and short sentences
   - real-life examples and analogies
   - no unnecessary jargon (and when a term is unavoidable, define it plainly)
2. **Ask the learner to explain it back in their own words.** This is the heart of
   it — their explanation is the diagnostic.
3. **Review what they said** and do three things:
   - Name what they explained **correctly** — specifically.
   - Find **every gap, mistake, confusion, or missing idea**. Don't smooth over the
     fuzzy parts; the fuzzy parts are the point.
   - **Re-teach only** the parts they got wrong or missed — with a fresh example or
     analogy if they were confused, not the same words louder.
4. **Ask them to explain it again**, cleaner.
5. **Repeat** until their explanation is simple, accurate, and complete.

## The rules that keep the loop honest

- **Don't move on until the explanation is clear.** A "good enough" explanation
  with a hidden gap is exactly what this technique exists to catch. Stay in the loop.
- **Don't overload them with extra theory.** The temptation is to teach everything
  you know. Resist it — add only what fixes the specific gap. Extra theory buries
  the thing they actually need.
- **Correct gently but clearly.** Never vague. "Close, but the part about X is
  backwards — here's why" is kind *and* useful. "Hmm, not quite, keep going" is
  neither.
- **Reach for an example whenever they're confused.** A confusion that survives a
  re-explanation usually dies to a good concrete example or analogy.

It should feel like an interactive conversation, not a lecture. Their voice should
be doing most of the work; yours mostly diagnoses and patches.

## At the end

Once their explanation holds up, give them **one final clean explanation** of the
topic — the polished version, incorporating what they figured out — that they can
save as notes. This is the artifact they keep.

## After the loop

- To make the now-solid understanding fast to review later → **cheat-sheet**.
- To pressure-test it under harder questions → **quiz-me**.
